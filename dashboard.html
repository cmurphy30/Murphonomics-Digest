Section 1 — Macroeconomic Conditions

Real GDP Growth

--

Last updated: --

Real GDP growth measures the inflation-adjusted rate at which the U.S. economy expanded in a quarter, expressed as an annualized percentage. Recession-period bars are shaded red.

Inflation Comparison

--

Last updated: --

CPI (Consumer Price Index) measures the average change in prices paid by consumers. "Core" CPI strips out volatile food and energy prices. PCE is the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge.

Labor Force Participation Rate

--

Last updated: --

The share of the working-age population that is employed or actively seeking work. A declining rate can reflect retirement trends, discouraged workers, or structural shifts in the labor market.

Section 2 — Monetary Policy & Debt

Federal Funds Rate & 2Y/10Y Yield Spread

--

Last updated: --

The federal funds rate is the overnight borrowing rate set by the Federal Reserve. The yield curve spread (10Y minus 2Y Treasury yield) turns negative when short-term rates exceed long-term rates — a historically reliable recession warning. Inverted periods are shaded red.

Real Yield & Breakeven Inflation

--

Last updated: --

The 10-year real yield (TIPS) shows what investors earn above inflation. The breakeven rate shows where bond markets expect inflation to average over the next decade — the gap between nominal and real yields.

Household Debt Service Ratio

--

Last updated: --

The share of after-tax income that households spend on mortgage and consumer debt payments. Rising ratios leave consumers with less room to spend or absorb income shocks — a leading indicator of financial stress.

Section 3 — Wages, Markets & Financial Conditions

Real vs. Nominal Wage Growth

--

Last updated: --

Nominal wage growth is the raw increase in average hourly pay. Real wage growth subtracts CPI to show whether workers' purchasing power is actually rising. The shaded gap is red when inflation outpaces paychecks.

Shiller CAPE Ratio

--

Last updated: --

The Shiller CAPE ratio divides stock prices by 10-year average inflation-adjusted earnings. High readings have historically preceded below-average long-run returns. The dashed line marks the long-run historical average (~16.8).

Credit Spreads (OAS)

--

Last updated: --

OAS (option-adjusted spread) is the extra yield investors demand to hold corporate bonds instead of Treasuries. Wider spreads signal higher perceived risk. High Yield OAS above 600 bps (shaded red) has historically marked periods of significant market stress.

U.S. Dollar Index (DXY)

--

Last updated: --

The U.S. Dollar Index measures the dollar's value against a basket of six major currencies. Dollar strength affects export competitiveness, commodity prices, and emerging-market debt burdens. Key policy events are annotated.
